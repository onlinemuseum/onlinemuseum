A history of historiography: from a national to a global, widespread and multi-disciplinary scope

Published: 10 May 2021

An overview of events from the Falklands War.

Introduction

  Historic texts are dependent upon the context in which they were written. The writing of a text is subject to the accessibility of sources, conventions within a discipline and that which has been written before. At the start of the twentieth century historical texts had a narrow scope and predominantly centralized the West. Contemporary societal mindsets such as Eurocentrism were reflected in history writing. As societal mindsets changed, historiography transformed too. From the 1930s onwards, alternative works such as Fernand Braudel's A History of Civilizations and William H. McNeill's The Rise of the West became increasingly influential in the academic community.

  Those who later became known as 'world historians' challenged traditional conventions by incorporating non-historical disciplines and by covering extensive spaces and times in their works. World history writing gradually manifested itself in the history community due to the changing society, globalization and efforts of movements such as the Annales school. The incremental development of historiography concurrent with long-term societal change is reflected by differences between world historical texts. McNeill's The Rise of the West from 1963 and Yuval N. Harrari's Sapiens from 2014 differ considerably from each other. This essay investigates how historiography has developed since the emergence of world history.
